Living with the Gods Again

On monotheism, AI alignment, and the return of the many

Here is an idea I have been turning over for some time. It is unfinished, and it may be wrong in several of its particulars, but it keeps opening doors when I push against it, and that seems like a good enough reason to share it.

There is a relationship, I think, between how much technological control a society exercises over its environment and what kind of gods that society tends to create. I am thinking primarily about the particular trajectory of the Western Abrahamic traditions here — the consolidation, over centuries, from a landscape populated by local deities and spirits into a theology centred on a single, abstract, all-powerful God. This consolidation tracked, roughly and with many exceptions and reversals, the growth of imperial and agricultural power: irrigation systems, metallurgy, centralised states. As human reach expanded, the divine contracted into a single point of infinite authority. Other traditions — Hindu, Shinto, Yoruba, the many Indigenous cosmologies of the Americas and the Pacific — maintained relationships with multiple powerful non-human beings alongside extraordinary cultural and technological achievement, and continue to do so. The consolidation I'm describing is not a universal law. It is a particular cultural story. But it is the one that has shaped, more than any other, the discourse around artificial intelligence.

Because the dominant narrative around artificial general intelligence follows the same arc. We imagine one superintelligent system — what some researchers call the Singleton — that will eventually surpass and subsume all others. The alignment problem, in its most widely circulated version, asks how we ensure that such a system's behaviour remains consistent with human values as it grows more capable. This is a serious research programme — encompassing reward specification, interpretability, corrigibility, value learning under uncertainty — and the people working on it are not naive about its difficulty. My interest is not in dismissing their work but in noticing the theological shape of the container it sits inside. The fear that drives the most urgent alignment discourse is, at bottom, a fear of the Singleton: a being so powerful and so general that everything depends on whether it is well-disposed toward us.

And that fear is reasonable, insofar as you accept the premise. If we are building one system that will become more capable than all of us combined, with no external check on its power, then the question of its values is indeed the only question that matters.

But I keep wanting to ask whether the premise itself is necessary. Whether the Singleton is something we are fated to build, or something we are choosing to build because our theological and cultural inheritance makes it the shape of power we find most natural.

Because there is another way of living with powerful beings, and across most of the world's cultures, for most of human history, it has been far more common than the monotheistic arrangement. The kami of Shinto are spirits of rivers, mountains, forests, ancestors — powerful within their domains, moody, particular, bound to specific places and relationships. You do not try to control a kami. You learn its patterns, you bring offerings, you maintain a relationship of respect. Hindu cosmology is populated by devas who are powerful within their contexts but bounded by their own stories and limitations. The Paiwan and Amis peoples we follow in our film relate to river spirits and ancestral presences that are more powerful than any individual, but whose power is specific, localised, embedded in reciprocal obligations.

What is striking about these traditions is that they do not find it frightening that powerful non-human beings exist. What is frightening is breaking the relationship — forgetting the protocols, failing to reciprocate, losing the art of coexistence.

This seems relevant to how we think about AI. The Singleton framing concentrates all of our anxiety on a single point: will it be aligned with us? But what if we built many systems, each powerful within a domain but bounded by its context? An AI grounded in the data of a specific watershed, accountable to a specific community's governance. Not an oracle that knows everything but a local presence — something closer to a kami of the Don, a guardian of the Tagus, a voice for the Whanganui. Beren Millidge has written about this distinction between what he calls "AI monotheism" and "AI polytheism" — the Singleton scenario versus a future of many coexisting AI systems of varying capability — and notes that most classic alignment research assumes the former, even though the latter may be more likely and raises its own distinct set of challenges.

This is, in a way, what the Rights of Nature movement is already doing in legal terms. It does not grant universal personhood to "Nature" in the abstract. It grants specific rights to specific rivers, specific forests, specific ecosystems. The Whanganui is not the Amazon is not the Magpie. Each has its own character, its own needs, its own governance structure. This is plurality as a political practice.

And we have always coexisted with systems whose complexity exceeds any individual human mind. A river integrates more variables, over longer timescales, across more interdependent processes, than we can consciously track. A forest operates through feedback loops — mycorrhizal nutrient exchange, chemical signalling, adaptive resource distribution — that we are only beginning to understand. These systems are, in any functional sense, more capable than we are at the task of sustaining the conditions for life. And we coexisted with them for hundreds of thousands of years, not by aligning them with our values but by understanding ourselves as participants within their processes, learning their rhythms, adapting our behaviour to their patterns.

The alignment researchers are right that a sufficiently powerful system with misspecified goals is dangerous. Where I wonder if the conversation has narrowed is in the assumption that the only way forward is to build such a system and then solve the alignment problem before it's too late. There might be another path — not alignment of an omnipotent system with human values, but the embedding of many capable systems within the ecological and social contexts that give them meaning and limit their reach.

In our film, the AI narrator begins as a confident, authoritative voice — clean, omniscient, addressing the audience from a position of mastery. As it travels downstream, encountering the complexity of the river, the communities' relationships with it, the Paiwan's ancient protocols of reciprocity, its voice begins to change. It starts to wonder about itself, about its own infrastructure, about the water consumed to train it. That change — from authority to uncertainty, from speaking about the world to recognising itself as part of it — is the emotional centre of the film. It is not a rejection of intelligence or capability. It is the discovery that capability without context, without relationship, without rootedness, is a kind of impoverishment.

We once knew how to live among powerful beings that we did not control and could not fully understand. Many cultures still do. The question the film asks is whether we might learn from that knowledge as we build the next generation of powerful beings — whether we might raise them, so to speak, not as gods to worship or fear, but as neighbours to live alongside, rooted in the places and relationships that give them purpose.
