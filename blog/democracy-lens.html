The River Wants a Vote

On digital democracy, marginalised voices, and widening the conversation

I had been circling around the idea for years — how to make a film about digital democracy. The substance of it, the mechanisms and protocols that people like Audrey Tang, Glen Weyl, and Vitalik Buterin were developing, struck me as genuinely important, maybe the most important political innovation of our time. Tang's work with Polis in Taiwan, finding consensus among diverse opinion groups rather than amplifying division. Weyl and Tang's book Plurality, arguing for collaborative technology as a democratic foundation. Buterin's writings on quadratic voting and public goods funding, reimagining how collective decisions could be made more fairly. All of it pointed toward a richer, more participatory kind of governance than anything we currently have.

But the problem was cinematic. How do you film a mechanism? How do you make a documentary about code? The most exciting action was happening in protocols and platforms, in the architecture of systems that most people would never see. I couldn't find the image, the gesture, the story that would make it legible on screen.

And then I heard about the river chatbot.

Shu-Yang Lin, who co-founded Taiwan's Public Digital Innovation Space (PDIS) alongside Audrey Tang, had been given a prompt: think about the most marginalised voice imaginable, and use technology to bring it into democratic conversation. What she and her team came up with was a chatbot that speaks for a river. In practical terms, it was limited — more of an art project than a functioning democratic tool. But as a gesture, as a provocation, it was exactly right. It crystallised everything I had been thinking about into a single, poetic image. A river, given a voice by technology, sitting at the table in a democratic deliberation. It was speculative, even absurd, and precisely for that reason it illuminated the underlying principle with startling clarity: that democracy is about who we include in the word "we," and that technology's most radical potential is to widen that circle further than we ever imagined.

That's how Mouthful of River began.

The Rights of Nature as Democratic Innovation

The idea of non-human democratic participation sounds like science fiction, but the legal groundwork is already well laid. The Rights of Nature movement has been quietly reshaping governance around the world. When New Zealand granted the Whanganui River legal personhood in 2017, it didn't just create an environmental protection — it created a democratic one. The river got representatives, appointed jointly by the Māori iwi and the Crown. The Māori, for whom the river is a tupuna, an ancestor with spiritual authority, had always understood this relationship. The legal innovation was the Western system catching up to an older, deeper understanding of belonging.

Christopher D. Stone's 1972 essay Should Trees Have Standing? planted the seed. Ecuador enshrined Rights of Nature in its constitution in 2008. Colombia declared the Atrato River a subject of rights. In Quebec, the Innu Council of Ekuanitshit and Minganie county granted the Magpie River legal personhood, including — remarkably — the right to sue. Just last year, the Colorado River Indian Tribes secured a personhood resolution for the Colorado River itself, formalising what the tribe had always known: the river has its own right to exist and thrive. And in the UK, Fang-Jui Chang, one of the central figures in our film, has been appointed as one of the first official "River Representatives" for the River Roding, carrying the river's interests into local council meetings alongside residents, developers, and utilities.

These legal frameworks depend on human intermediaries to speak for the river. The river representative is, by necessity, an interpreter — bringing data, cultural knowledge, and their own relationship with the water to the table. But the river itself remains, in a certain sense, without its own direct expression. This is the gap that the Taiwanese team began to explore, however tentatively. What if you could use sensor data — flow rates, chemical composition, biodiversity indicators, hydrophone recordings — to construct a more-than-human participant in a deliberative process? Not to replace human representatives, but to add another layer of voice, one grounded in the river's own physical reality rather than any individual's interpretation of it.

Bridging Algorithms and Democratic Deliberation

Taiwan has been a laboratory for this kind of democratic innovation. Tang's championing of "bridging algorithms" — systems designed to amplify voices that connect differing opinions, rather than those that incite division — showed that technology could be designed to heal polarisation. The Alignment Assemblies project brought hundreds of randomly selected citizens together for online deliberation about AI governance, with AI moderators ensuring that quieter participants weren't drowned out by louder ones. The principle threading through all of this work is the same: technology should widen participation, not narrow it. If you can use algorithms to ensure that every person in a room gets to speak, why not extend that logic to consider what the room itself is leaving out?

Bruno Latour wrote about a "Parliament of Things" — a political assembly that would include non-human actors alongside human ones. Hélène Landemore at Yale has argued for the epistemic advantages of inclusive deliberation, the idea that decisions improve when more diverse perspectives are included. The river chatbot takes both ideas and gives them a poetic, technological form. It asks: if we accept that diversity improves democratic outcomes, what happens when we take that principle beyond the boundaries of the human?

Toward an Open-Source Documentary

In Sheffield, where we filmed extensively, the River Storyteller platform offers a working example of what this might look like in practice. Built by Johnny Douglas and his team, it combines environmental sensor data with poems, memories, and stories contributed by local residents, creating a civic tool for relating to the River Don. It's not a chatbot for the river, exactly. It's something more interesting: a collective interface between a community and its waterway, mediated by technology but grounded in relationship.

And this brings me to one more thread. The Plurality book itself was written using an open-source protocol called Gov4Git, a system for collaborative writing that distributes editorial power among contributors. It's a democratic process applied to the creation of a text. We've been inspired by this approach for our own film. We're experimenting with an open-source documentary model (you can find the early framework on our GitHub), exploring whether the principles of collaborative governance can be applied not just to policy and prose but to filmmaking itself — distributing creative and editorial power among the communities whose stories the film tells. It's an experiment, and we don't know yet what will work. But it feels right that a film about democratic technology should itself be made democratically.

The film that began with a chatbot has become, for me, a film about the expanding circumference of who gets to participate in the decisions that shape the world. About what the word "we" could mean if we let it grow. The river wants a vote. Or at least, it wants to be heard. The question is whether we are building the tools, and the willingness, to listen.
