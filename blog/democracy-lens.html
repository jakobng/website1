The River Wants a Vote

On digital democracy, marginalised voices, and widening the conversation

I had been circling around the idea for years — how to make a film about digital democracy. The substance of it, the mechanisms and protocols that people like Audrey Tang, Glen Weyl, and Vitalik Buterin were developing, struck me as genuinely important, maybe the most important political innovation of our time. Tang's work with Polis in Taiwan, finding consensus among diverse opinion groups rather than amplifying division. Weyl and Tang's book Plurality, arguing for collaborative technology as a democratic foundation. Buterin's writings on quadratic voting and public goods funding, reimagining how collective decisions could be made more fairly. All of it pointed toward a richer, more participatory kind of governance than anything we currently have.

But the problem was cinematic. How do you film a mechanism? How do you make a documentary about code? The most exciting action was happening in protocols and platforms, in the architecture of systems that most people would never see. I couldn't find the image, the gesture, the story that would make it legible on screen.

And then I heard about the river chatbot.

Shu-Yang Lin, who co-founded Taiwan's Public Digital Innovation Space (PDIS) alongside Audrey Tang, had been given a prompt: think about the most marginalised voice imaginable, and use technology to bring it into democratic conversation. What she and her team came up with was a chatbot that speaks for a river. In practical terms, it was limited — more of an art project than a functioning democratic tool, a provocation rather than a prototype. But as a gesture it was exactly right. It crystallised everything I had been thinking about into a single, poetic image: a river, given a voice by technology, sitting at the table in a democratic deliberation. Speculative, even absurd, and precisely for that reason it illuminated the underlying principle with startling clarity — that democracy is fundamentally about who we include in the word "we," and that technology's most radical potential is to widen that circle further than we had imagined possible.

That's how Mouthful of River began.

The idea of non-human participation in governance sounds like science fiction, but the legal groundwork has been quietly building for decades. When New Zealand granted the Whanganui River legal personhood in 2017, it created a democratic innovation as much as an environmental one. The river got representatives, appointed jointly by the Māori iwi and the Crown. The Māori, for whom the river is a tupuna — an ancestor with spiritual authority — had always understood this relationship. The Te Awa Tupua Act formalised it in terms the Western legal system could recognise.

Christopher D. Stone's 1972 essay Should Trees Have Standing? planted the seed. Ecuador enshrined Rights of Nature in its constitution in 2008. Colombia declared the Atrato River a subject of rights. In Quebec, the Innu Council of Ekuanitshit and Minganie county granted the Magpie River legal personhood, including the right to sue. Last year, the Colorado River Indian Tribes secured a personhood resolution for the Colorado River itself. And in the UK, Fang-Jui Chang, one of the central figures in our film, has been appointed as one of the first official "River Representatives" for the River Roding, carrying the river's interests into local council meetings.

These legal frameworks depend on human intermediaries to speak for the river. The representative is, by necessity, an interpreter — bringing data, cultural knowledge, and their own relationship with the water to the table. The river itself has no direct expression in the proceedings. This is the gap that the Taiwanese team began to explore, however tentatively. What if sensor data — flow rates, chemical composition, biodiversity indicators, hydrophone recordings — could be woven into a more-than-human participant in a deliberative process? Not to replace human representatives, but to add another layer of input, one grounded in the river's own physical reality rather than any single person's reading of it.

Taiwan has been a laboratory for democratic innovation of this kind. Tang's Alignment Assemblies brought hundreds of randomly selected citizens together for online deliberation about AI governance, with AI moderators managing speaking time to ensure that quieter participants weren't drowned out. Polis, the platform used in over thirty policy cases, has shown that it is possible to build digital spaces where people find common ground rather than polarise. These are still experiments — contested, partial, with real limitations — but they point toward a different relationship between technology and democratic participation than the one social media has given us. If you can use algorithms to surface the connective tissue between disagreeing humans, the river chatbot asks what it might mean to extend that logic beyond the human entirely.

Bruno Latour wrote about a "Parliament of Things" — a political assembly that would include non-human actors alongside human ones. Hélène Landemore at Yale has argued that decisions improve when more diverse perspectives are included in deliberation. The river chatbot gives both ideas a poetic, technological form, and asks what happens when we take the principle of inclusive deliberation beyond the boundaries of those who can speak for themselves.

In Sheffield, where we filmed extensively, the River Storyteller platform offers a working sketch of what this might look like. Built by Johnny Douglas and his team, it combines environmental sensor data with poems, memories, and stories contributed by local residents, creating a civic interface for relating to the River Don. It's not a chatbot for the river. It's something more nuanced: a space where scientific data and human testimony and cultural memory coexist, layered together, none fully authoritative on its own.

This layering of voices is also part of how we are trying to make the film itself. The Plurality book was written using an open-source protocol called Gov4Git, a system for collaborative writing that distributes editorial power among contributors — a democratic process applied to the creation of a text. We've been inspired by this approach and are experimenting with something similar for Mouthful of River, exploring whether the principles of collaborative governance can extend to filmmaking, distributing creative power among the communities whose stories the film tells. The early framework is on GitHub. It feels right, if difficult, that a film about democratic technology should itself grapple with the question of who gets to shape it.
