The Oldest Superintelligence

On ecosystems, extraction, and learning to listen again

There is a song called milikir that the Cepo' Pangcah (Amis) people sing when travelling by raft along the Siuguluan River in eastern Taiwan. The word means something like "shooting the rapids." The song has no fixed lyrics, only vocables — improvised additions that name features of the landscape, encourage the crew, sometimes make jokes to stare down fear. A woman named ciMeilice sang it from a zodiac raft one afternoon, telling imagined young men to be strong, they are bringing home wood and bamboo to build houses in the village. The artist ciRahic sang the same melody with a different weight: he once sang it during a typhoon, a landslide bearing down on him, pleading with the river for his life. And the songwriter Anu Kaliting Sadipongan recorded a version for an album, bringing the ancestral journey and the river's presence into a studio and from there into the ears of people who may never have stood on the Siuguluan's banks.

What interests me about milikir is that it works as a technology of relationship. It carries the river into the singer and the singer into the river. It is portable — it travels with the Pangcah, keeping the relationship alive even far from the water, insisting that the river remains central to their world even as settler infrastructure — highways, dams, tourist enterprises — works to make it peripheral. The ethnographer DJ Hatfield has written beautifully about how milikir conjures what he calls a "shared subjunctive," a space in which singers and listeners reclaim the river as a life-giving centre.

Mouthful of River is a film about many things, but at its root it is about two different orientations toward a river. On one side, there are communities and traditions that understand the river as a being, a relative, a source of life with whom you maintain an ongoing, reciprocal relationship. On the other, an industrial logic that treats the river as an input — water to be diverted, a coolant for machines, an externality to be managed.

We see this playing out with painful clarity in Taiwan. TSMC, the world's largest semiconductor manufacturer, consumes over 150,000 tonnes of water per day — roughly ten per cent of Hsinchu City's entire supply. During the severe drought of 2021, the worst in more than fifty years, the government rationed water for farms and households while deploying fleets of water trucks to keep the chip factories running. Taiwan's semiconductor industry is genuinely existential for the country's economy and geopolitical survival, and I don't think anyone involved in those decisions took them lightly. But the shape of the dilemma itself — that a society could arrive at a point where it must choose between irrigating its fields and manufacturing its microprocessors — tells you something about the kind of world that has been built and the assumptions it runs on.

The Tayal people of Hsinchu County, whose ancestral territories surround the reservoirs and rivers feeding the science park, live inside this dilemma daily. Research in Jianshi Township has shown that Tayal systems for governing water persist — rooted in communal custodianship, in a worldview where water itself has active agency in the governance arrangement, where managing a river is understood as a relationship among human and non-human participants. These systems are not relics. They are living practices, under immense and growing pressure from the expansion of the industry that surrounds them.

And it is not only in Taiwan. In rural Spain, we follow Aurora Gómez and her activist group Tu Nube Seca Mi Río — "Your Cloud Dries My River" — as they organise resistance against Amazon's proposed data centres in drought-prone Aragón. In Memphis, Tennessee, Elon Musk's xAI installed dozens of gas turbines to power its Colossus supercomputer without obtaining air permits, and the Southern Environmental Law Center found the facility had likely become the largest industrial source of smog-forming pollution in the city — in a predominantly Black neighbourhood where the cancer risk is already four times the national average. Residents learned about the turbines not from the company or their officials but from an advocacy group's press release. The pattern recurs: extraction presented as progress, concentrated in communities with the least capacity to resist it, and kept from public view for as long as possible.

There is something in this logic that flattens. It reduces a river to a volume of water, a community to a workforce, a landscape to a site for infrastructure. Everything is made legible to a single metric of value, and whatever cannot be made legible is treated as though it does not exist. The wider ecological crisis — collapsing biodiversity, destabilised climate, dying rivers — follows from this flattening as naturally as drought follows deforestation.

Robin Wall Kimmerer, the Potawatomi botanist, describes plants as our oldest teachers, beings that have figured out, over hundreds of millions of years, how to exist in relationship. They make food from light and give it away. For Kimmerer, ecological consciousness begins with acknowledging our reciprocal relationship with the rest of the living world — receiving its gifts with open eyes and learning to give our own gifts in return.

We begin our film in southern Taiwan, among a Paiwan community. Before taking stones from the riverbank to build a house, the Paiwan ask the river's permission, promising to return what was borrowed. This is a protocol for living within a system that sustains you. It encodes an understanding that the river's generosity is real but not unlimited, that taking requires asking, and that the asking itself is part of what keeps the relationship intact.

The Tayal concept of gaga — an unwritten moral code governing all aspects of life, passed down through generations — extends to their relationship with water in ways that challenge the assumptions of modern resource management. In Tayal governance, water is not a passive substance to be allocated but an active participant in the arrangement, with its own agency and its own claims.

An ecosystem processes information across vast scales — integrating geology, biology, chemistry, and climate into adaptive, self-regulating patterns. James Bridle, in Ways of Being, argues that what we recognise as intelligence may be a property of complex systems far beyond brains, from slime moulds solving mazes to forests redistributing resources through mycorrhizal networks. Whether or not we want to call this "intelligence" — and reasonable people disagree about whether the word should stretch that far — the functional sophistication of these systems is extraordinary, and it long predates anything we have engineered.

The paradox at the heart of our film is that we are developing increasingly powerful technologies to "listen" to rivers — hydrophones, environmental sensors, machine learning systems that can detect patterns invisible to human perception — while the infrastructure powering these technologies consumes the rivers themselves. The listening devices run on electricity from the same grid that powers the data centres that drain the watershed. The technology that might give a river a voice depends on an industry that is making rivers go quiet.

This is not a problem that better engineering alone can resolve. It is a problem of orientation — of what we understand ourselves to be in relation to the systems that sustain us. The Pangcah sing to the river and the river carries them. The Paiwan ask permission before taking stones. The Tayal govern their water collectively, recognising the water's own part in the arrangement. These are governance systems that have sustained communities and ecosystems for millennia, and they are under pressure not because they have failed but because a more powerful system, built on different assumptions, has decided that the land and water they care for is needed for something else.
