The Oldest Superintelligence

On ecosystems, extraction, and learning to listen again

There is a song called milikir that the Cepo' Pangcah (Amis) people sing when travelling by raft along the Siuguluan River in eastern Taiwan. The word means something like "shooting the rapids." The song has no fixed lyrics, only vocables — improvised additions that name features of the landscape, encourage the crew, sometimes joke about the dangers ahead. In one version, a woman named ciMeilice sings from a zodiac raft, telling the young men to be strong, they are bringing home wood and bamboo to build houses in the village. In another, the artist ciRahic sings the same melody, but with a different urgency: he once sang it to the river during a typhoon, a landslide bearing down on him, pleading for his life.

What strikes me about milikir is that it is a technology of relationship. The song carries the river into the singer and the singer into the river. It is portable — Anu Kaliting Sadipongan recorded a version for an album in 2013, bringing the ancestral journey and the river's presence into a recording studio, and from there into the lives of listeners who may never have stood on the Siuguluan's banks. The song is a way of keeping the relationship alive even when you are far from the water. It is a way of insisting that the river remains central to the Pangcah world, even as settler infrastructure — highways, dams, tourist enterprises — works to make it peripheral.

This is not the kind of knowledge that fits neatly into a data dashboard.

The Logic of Extraction

Mouthful of River is a film about many things, but at its root it is about the distance between two ways of relating to a river. On one side, there are the communities and traditions that understand the river as a being, a relative, a source of life with whom you maintain an ongoing, reciprocal relationship. On the other, there is an industrial logic that sees the river as a resource — water to be diverted, a coolant for machines, an externality to be managed.

We see this logic playing out with devastating clarity in Taiwan, where TSMC, the world's largest semiconductor manufacturer, consumes over 150,000 tonnes of water per day. During the severe drought of 2021 — the worst in more than fifty years — the government rationed water for farms and households while dispatching fleets of water trucks to keep the chip factories running. The message was unmistakable: in a contest between a river and a microprocessor, the microprocessor wins. The Tayal people of Hsinchu County, whose ancestral territories surround the reservoirs and rivers that feed the science park, have long understood what this means. Their systems for governing water — rooted in communal custodianship, in what scholars have described as governance among non-human and human agencies in a more-than-human world — persist, but under immense pressure from the thirst of an industry that accounts for roughly ten per cent of the city's entire water supply.

And it is not only in Taiwan. In rural Spain, we follow Aurora Gómez and her activist group Tu Nube Seca Mi Río — "Your Cloud Dries My River" — as they organise resistance against Amazon's proposed data centres in the drought-prone regions of Aragón and Castilla-La Mancha. In Memphis, Tennessee, Elon Musk's xAI installed dozens of unpermitted gas turbines to power its Colossus supercomputer, and the Southern Environmental Law Center found the facility had become likely the largest industrial source of smog-forming pollution in the entire city — built in a predominantly Black neighbourhood already burdened by decades of environmental injustice. Residents of the Boxtown neighbourhood, where the cancer risk is four times the national average, learned about the turbines from a press release, not from the company or their elected officials. The pattern is always the same: extraction presented as progress, concentrated in communities that are least equipped to resist it, and shielded from public oversight for as long as possible.

There is something flattening about this logic. It reduces a river to a volume of water, a community to a labour force, a landscape to a site. It is the logic of what you might call singularity in the cultural sense — the narrowing of the world into a single metric of value, a single direction of flow from the many toward the one. Everything is made legible to the market, and what cannot be made legible is erased. It is the opposite of the plurality that the film tries to hold.

Reciprocity and the Oldest Teachers

Robin Wall Kimmerer, the Potawatomi botanist, describes plants as our oldest teachers, beings that have figured out, over hundreds of millions of years, how to live reciprocally. They make food from light and give it away. They know how to exist in relationship. For Kimmerer, the awakening of ecological consciousness requires acknowledging and celebrating our reciprocal relationship with the rest of the living world — receiving its gifts with open eyes and learning to give our own gifts in return.

We begin our film in southern Taiwan, among a Paiwan community. Before taking stones from the riverbank to build a house, the Paiwan ask the river's permission, promising to return what was borrowed. This is not an archaic ritual. It is a protocol for living within a system that sustains you. It encodes an understanding that the river is not raw material but a being to be negotiated with, whose generosity is not unlimited and whose patience is not infinite.

The Tayal people's concept of gaga — an unwritten moral code governing all aspects of life, passed down through generations, emphasising harmony and mutual respect — extends to their relationship with water. Research in Jianshi Township has shown that in Tayal governance, water itself has active agency; governing water is about adaptive relationships among non-human and human entities, about communally sharing custodianship of something that belongs to no one and sustains everyone.

Ecosystem as Intelligence

An ecosystem is, in this sense, a form of intelligence far older and more sophisticated than anything we have built. A river system processes information across thousands of kilometres, integrating geology, biology, chemistry, and climate into a coherent, adaptive whole. A forest communicates through mycorrhizal networks, redistributing resources, signalling danger. James Bridle, in Ways of Being, argues that intelligence is a property of complex systems everywhere, not a quality unique to brains. The question is whether we have the perceptual apparatus to recognise it, or whether we have trained ourselves, over centuries, to see only resources.

The paradox at the heart of our film is that we are now developing extraordinarily sophisticated technologies to "listen" to rivers — hydrophones, environmental sensors, machine learning systems that can detect patterns invisible to human perception — while the infrastructure that powers these technologies is itself consuming the rivers. The technology that might give the river a voice requires data centres that drain it. The listening devices are powered by the same industry that is making the rivers go silent.

This is not a problem that can be solved by better technology alone. It is a problem of relationship. The Pangcah sing to the river and the river carries them. The Paiwan ask permission before taking stones. The Tayal govern their water collectively, recognising the water's own agency in the arrangement. These are not quaint traditions from a pre-modern world. They are technologies of attention, protocols of care, governance systems that have sustained communities and ecosystems for millennia. And they are under threat not because they have failed but because a more powerful system, built on extraction rather than reciprocity, has decided they are in the way.

The wider ecological crisis — the collapsing biodiversity, the destabilised climate, the dying rivers — is not a failure of nature. It is the consequence of a culture that forgot how to listen, and that built its most powerful technologies on the assumption that it didn't need to.
